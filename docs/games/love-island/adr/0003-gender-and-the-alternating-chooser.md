# Player gender is mechanical, and the alternating-chooser completes the Recoupling

The player **chooses their gender** at Season start, and gender is **mechanical, not
cosmetic**. It sets three things: the pool you couple *from*, your side of the Casa
Amor split, and — via the show's **alternating-chooser rule** — your position at
each Recoupling.

## The alternating-chooser rule

Recouplings alternate who holds the power:

- **Your gender chooses** → you have **agency** (keep your Partner, Bond intact; or
  switch, Bond resets — see ADR-0002). **No Dumped risk** this round: you are the
  one picking.
- **The other gender chooses** → you are **passive and exposed**. The `Bond ≥ floor
  OR Public ≥ floor` survival check *is* "does anyone pick you": a strong Bond means
  your Partner re-picks you, a strong Public means someone else does. Fail both →
  single → `Dumped`.

This is why gender is load-bearing rather than flavour: it is the missing half of
the Recoupling mechanic from ADR-0002. It gives the Season a **risk rhythm** —
alternating safe-and-choosing rounds with exposed-and-waiting ones — and makes
Bond/Public bite precisely when you are the chosen, not the chooser.

## Hetero format for launch

Launch models the **traditional heterosexual format**: you couple from the opposite
gender, and the Cast is built to pair with you. Queer/fluid formats are a **clean
future Season variant** — the *engine* is indifferent (it is only Cast composition
+ pairing rules in the pack), so deferring loses nothing structural while avoiding a
doubling of launch content and both-orientation copy before the format proves out.

## Why this is hard to reverse

Gender determines Cast composition (a symmetric roster you pair against), the Casa
Amor split, and the chooser/chosen framing of every Recoupling and its copy.
Retrofitting orientation choice or a non-gendered pairing model later touches the
Cast, the coupling rules, and a large share of the deck's framing — so the
launch commitment is pinned here.
