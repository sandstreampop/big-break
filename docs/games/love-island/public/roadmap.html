The villa game · the plan, in plain words

Where our little dating-show game goes next 🌴

We built a game about surviving a season on a reality dating show. About twenty people have played the test version. This page walks through what we learned — and the plan to make it something people come back to every day. No game-industry jargon, promise.

1The game, in one minute

You play one contestant on a Love Island–style TV show. The whole game is played by swiping cards left or right, like choosing between two replies in a conversation. Each card is a moment from the show — a date, a challenge, a juicy rumour, a dreaded text message — and each choice nudges your story: how strong your couple is, how much the public likes you, how close you are to losing your head.

One playthrough is one season — about ten minutes on your phone. You pick what you're really there for: winning the public's vote, finding actual love, or building a following. Get there by the finale and you win. Or get dumped from the villa and go home in tears. Then you play another season, and it all goes differently.

The pitch in one sentence: “Reigns, but you're in the villa — and every choice is free.” (Reigns is a famous card-swiping game. “Free” is a dig at the official Love Island game, which charges real money for the good choices. We never will.)

2Where things stand today

The surprising part: the game is finished, in the sense that everything we designed got built. There's a scheming Rival with their own secrets, a mid-season loyalty trial, a gossip system where you collect and spend secrets, and a sarcastic narrator who comments on everything you do and never repeats himself.

3What we learned — three ways of looking

👀 Watching real players

The game quietly counts anonymous plays (no names, no personal data — just “someone swiped this card”). Twenty people opened the game; only five actually played a season. That's the loudest finding: most visitors bounce before their first swipe. The villa game doesn't introduce itself at all — you're thrown into the first card cold. Meanwhile, one person played forty-two seasons, which tells us the game can hook someone hard once it gets the chance.

Also: nearly everyone played on a phone. Whatever we build must feel great under a thumb.

🤖 Robot playtesters

Here's a trick human playtests can't do: we had the computer play ten thousand complete seasons in under a minute, making human-ish choices, and then studied the pile of results. Overall the game is fair — roughly one in three seasons ends in a win, which is the sweet spot we aim for. But the robots exposed three real imbalances (next section).

🌍 Looking around the market

  • The show is enormous right now. Love Island USA is breaking streaming records this very month, and its audience is young and phone-first — exactly the people who'd play a game like ours.
  • The official game's biggest complaint is paywalls. Players hate that the “good” choices cost money. Our game is free and always will be — that's not a limitation, it's the whole sales pitch.
  • Games people replay forever share one trick: every new run shows you something you haven't seen. Ours forgets everything between seasons — an obvious place to grow.
  • Small free web games still go viral in one specific way: a shared daily puzzle plus a spoiler-free results card you can paste into the group chat (the Wordle trick). Nobody has done that with a story game yet.
Wait — should we worry about the name “Love Island”?

Yes, eventually. “Love Island” is a trademark that ITV licenses to an official game. While we're a tiny free fan project nobody minds, but before we ever promote the game loudly, it needs its own name — keeping the reality-show format (recouplings, bombshells, “I've got a text!” — those are genre conventions, not property), just not the brand. Picking the new name is a human decision; preparing candidates is on the to-do list.

4The three problems worth fixing first

Problem 1 Being nice is too easy.

One of the three ways to win is leaving with real love. The robots showed that the “loyal” score you need for it basically fills itself up — in half of all seasons it hits the maximum. A meter that's always full isn't a choice anymore, it's decoration. Fix: make loyal choices count for a little less each, so staying true is something you do, not something that happens to you.

Problem 2 There's only one way to lose.

In ten thousand robot seasons, effectively every loss was the same loss: being single when couples re-form. The game also has a stress meter — the voice in your head — that looks scary and rises all season, but it ended a season twice in ten thousand tries. A threat that never bites is a broken promise to the player. Fix: give the stress meter real teeth (rarely, and with fair warning from the narrator).

Problem 3 The influencer path is starved.

The third way to win — turning villa fame into a following — fails far more often than the other two, because the charm points it needs mostly arrive as a consolation prize when things go badly. You can't choose your way to it. That's backwards, and it's the path the show's real audience loves most. Fix: let charm be earned by nailing challenges and big camera moments.

One more, for the connoisseurs: the wasted plot twist

The game's most dramatic designed moment — saving up a rival's dirty secret and detonating it at the couples ceremony — turns out to happen in about 1 season in 50, because the stars rarely align. A scene that good should fire far more often. Small tuning, big drama payoff.

5The plan: three bets, in order

Order matters. There's no point advertising a leaky bucket, so the bets stack: first make the game fair and welcoming, then give people a reason to return, then invite the world.

Bet 1 — Finish the fun 🔧

Fix the three imbalances above, and teach the game to say hello. The best idea here: the sarcastic narrator becomes your guide on your very first season — the show itself onboards new viewers through the voiceover, so the game should too. (“That meter? That's the voice in your head, babes. Keep an eye on it.”) No tutorial screens, no manual — the character does it.

Also in this bet: better play-counting instruments, so every later change can be measured instead of guessed at.

Bet 2 — The reason to come back tomorrow 📅

  • The Daily Villa. Every day, one shared season — same cast, same bombshells, same secrets for everyone. At the end you get a spoiler-free little results card (think emoji, not text) made for pasting into the group chat: “Day 33. Survived two ceremonies. Kept the secret. Left with real love.” Your friends see it and have to know what their partner would have done.
  • The villa remembers. Play a second season and your ex is still there — and remembers exactly how you left things. The narrator remembers too. Every rerun reveals new lines and grudges, which is the trick every endlessly-replayed game uses.
  • More people worth knowing. Partners get personalities (the sweetheart, the player, the slow-burner — each with their own secrets), and the villa finally gets the show's most underrated relationship: your best friend, your ride-or-die, who hears your gossip and has your back at the vote.
  • Things to unlock. Finish a season and unlock a new way to play — headline act: starting as the Bombshell, dropped into a villa where everyone's already coupled up.

Bet 3 — Then invite the world 📣

  • A front door that actually mentions the villa game (right now it hides behind the music game we built first, with no signpost).
  • The one-sentence pitch everywhere it counts.
  • A new name of our own (see the note above), then promotion.
  • Timing: the show's cultural peak is June–August every year. The smart play is to spend this year making Bets 1 and 2 true, and make the big push when the 2027 season hype arrives.

6The dream list 🌙

Bigger swings, deliberately parked until the bets above prove out:

  • Everyone can date everyone. The launch version models the classic boy–girl format. A version where you set who you're into is the most-requested feature in this whole genre — and it deserves its own proper design session, not a bolt-on.
  • A villa that lives without you. Right now the drama orbits you. Give the other islanders their own couples that form and shatter off-screen — so gossip can be about them — and the villa starts feeling alive. (Hand-written drama, mind — the industry tried improvising-AI characters last year and players hated the mush.)
  • Playable challenges. Tiny games for the show's party games — heart-rate challenge, the lie-detector test.
  • Look and feel. Reaction-shot artwork at the big verdicts, a buzz in your hand when a text arrives, confetti when you survive the ceremony.

7Who does what — the AI and the human 🤝

An unusual fact about this project: almost all of the plan can be executed by an AI working alone, because the project checks its own work — a robot referee replays thousands of seasons after every change, a taste-checker rejects writing that breaks the game's voice, and a battery of tests guards everything else. The AI writes, tunes, tests, and ships; the checks catch what slips.

🤖 The AI handles
  • All balance tuning (robot playtests are the judge)
  • Writing new scenes & narrator lines (taste-checked)
  • Building the daily mode, memory, unlocks
  • Nightly stats reports & a weekly digest
🧑‍🎨 Viktor decides
  • Taste: spot-checking ~10% of new writing
  • The new name, and when to go loud
  • Art direction (portrait style, sound)
  • Who gets invited to playtest

Rough deal: the AI ships continuously; the human spends about fifteen minutes a week on verdicts and decisions.
